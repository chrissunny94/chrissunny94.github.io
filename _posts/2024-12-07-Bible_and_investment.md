I have for many years wondered about Jesus's position on investing. Let us approach this topic objectively.

The Nature of Money
First and foremost, money is not a commodity; it is a tool for the exchange of value. Its purpose is to facilitate trade and provide a medium for economic activity.

Jesus and Speculation
From a Biblical perspective, Jesus appears to discourage speculative practices. This can be inferred from His teachings, which emphasize honest work, fair dealings, and stewardship of resources. Speculation, often driven by greed and uncertainty, contrasts sharply with these principles.

Gold and Silver as Standards
Historically, Gold and Silver have been seen as stable mediums of exchange. Their intrinsic value is derived from their scarcity, as they cannot be synthesized and must be mined. This makes them relatively immune to the speculative fluctuations that plague fiat currencies. In a way, their fixed supply reflects a divine order—offering stability and fairness in trade.

The Issue with Modern Money
Today, money, especially fiat currency, lacks intrinsic value. It is subject to inflation and speculation, eroding its reliability as a medium of exchange. A significant shift occurred when the U.S. dollar moved away from the Gold standard, transitioning into the era of the "petrodollar." This decoupling from a tangible standard introduced an environment ripe for speculative practices, diminishing the moral foundation of money as a tool for equitable exchange.

Jesus’ teachings on investing, as recorded in the Bible, are often symbolic and focus on spiritual and moral principles rather than financial advice. Here are some key passages and insights:

### 1. **Parable of the Talents (Matthew 25:14-30)**
This parable describes a master who entrusts his servants with money ("talents") before leaving on a journey. Two servants invest the money and double it, while the third buries it out of fear. The master commends the two who invested wisely and scolds the one who played it safe.

**Lesson**: Use your resources, talents, and opportunities to grow and contribute, rather than hiding them out of fear or inaction. God values effort and responsibility in stewarding what we have.

---

### 2. **Laying Up Treasures in Heaven (Matthew 6:19-21)**
Jesus says:
> "Do not store up for yourselves treasures on earth, where moths and rust destroy, and where thieves break in and steal. But store up for yourselves treasures in heaven..."

**Lesson**: Investing in spiritual growth, helping others, and living a life of love and service has eternal value, while material wealth is temporary.

---

### 3. **The Rich Fool (Luke 12:13-21)**
In this parable, a rich man stores up wealth for himself, thinking it will secure his future. God calls him a fool for not being "rich toward God," as he dies that night and cannot take his wealth with him.

**Lesson**: Accumulating wealth without generosity or a sense of purpose is futile. True wealth lies in relationships, generosity, and spiritual richness.

---

### 4. **Counting the Cost (Luke 14:28-30)**
Jesus asks:
> "Suppose one of you wants to build a tower. Won’t you first sit down and estimate the cost to see if you have enough money to complete it?"

**Lesson**: Plan and act wisely with the resources you have. Thoughtful preparation is a part of faithful stewardship.

---

### Key Takeaways for Modern Investing:
- **Stewardship**: Treat your resources as gifts to be used responsibly for the greater good.
- **Purpose**: Prioritize investments that align with your values and benefit others.
- **Generosity**: Share your wealth and support those in need.
- **Eternal Perspective**: Remember that material wealth is temporary; invest in what has lasting significance, such as relationships and character.

The Bible addresses the practice of **usury** (charging excessive or unfair interest on loans) in several passages, often condemning it, particularly when it exploits the poor or vulnerable. Here's what the Bible says about usury:

---

### 1. **Old Testament Laws on Usury**
- **Exodus 22:25**  
  > "If you lend money to one of my people among you who is needy, do not treat it like a business deal; charge no interest."

  **Lesson**: Lending to those in need should be an act of compassion, not a means to profit.

- **Leviticus 25:35-37**  
  > "If any of your fellow Israelites become poor and are unable to support themselves among you, help them as you would a foreigner or stranger, so they can continue to live among you. Do not take interest or any profit from them, but fear your God, so that they may continue to live among you."

  **Lesson**: The command emphasizes mercy and care for the poor, prohibiting interest on loans to fellow Israelites.

- **Deuteronomy 23:19-20**  
  > "Do not charge a fellow Israelite interest, whether on money or food or anything else that may earn interest. You may charge a foreigner interest, but not a fellow Israelite, so that the Lord your God may bless you in everything you put your hand to in the land you are entering to possess."

  **Lesson**: While interest could be charged to foreigners, lending to fellow Israelites was expected to be free of interest, fostering community solidarity.

---

### 2. **Condemnation of Exploitation**
- **Psalm 15:5**  
  > "Whoever does not lend money at interest and does not accept a bribe against the innocent. Whoever does these things will never be shaken."

  **Lesson**: Those who avoid usury are considered righteous and upright in God’s eyes.

- **Ezekiel 18:13**  
  > "He lends at interest and takes a profit. Will such a man live? He will not! Because he has done all these detestable things, he is to be put to death; his blood will be on his own head."

  **Lesson**: Charging excessive interest is seen as sinful and unjust.

---

### 3. **New Testament Perspective**
The New Testament does not directly address usury as the Old Testament does, but it reinforces principles of generosity and kindness in lending:
- **Luke 6:34-35**  
  > "And if you lend to those from whom you expect repayment, what credit is that to you? Even sinners lend to sinners, expecting to be repaid in full. But love your enemies, do good to them, and lend to them without expecting to get anything back."

  **Lesson**: Lending should come from a place of love and generosity, not greed or expectation of gain.

---

### Summary
- **Prohibited Exploitation**: Usury is condemned when it exploits the poor or creates an unjust burden.
- **Compassionate Lending**: Loans should be given out of kindness and care, especially to those in need, without charging interest.
- **Community and Equity**: The Bible promotes a sense of communal responsibility and fairness in economic practices.
